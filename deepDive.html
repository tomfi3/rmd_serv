Analysis of Survey Responses on Richmond Air Quality Action Plan

1. Other Approaches Suggested

Common Points Raised:

  • Reduce Car Usage: Suggestions to discourage private car use through higher parking charges, limiting parking availability, and promoting alternatives.
  • Promote Active Travel: Calls for more segregated cycle lanes, improved cycling infrastructure, and pedestrianized streets.
  • Enforce Anti-Idling Measures: Emphasis on strict enforcement against engine idling, especially near schools and level crossings.
  • Address Wood Burning Stoves: Concerns about pollution from wood burners and suggestions to ban or restrict their use.
  • Tackle Aircraft Pollution: Requests to address noise and air pollution from aircraft and oppose Heathrow expansion.
  • Improve Public Transport: Suggestions to expand bus routes, increase frequency, and electrify public transport.

Good Suggestions:

  • Implement higher charges for car parking to discourage car use.
  • Create dedicated, segregated cycle lanes with barriers for safety.
  • Provide free, covered bicycle parking and incentives for e-bike purchases.
  • Empower officers with more authority to enforce anti-idling laws.
  • Proactively reduce the use of wood-burning stoves in residential areas.
  • Install more air quality monitoring stations throughout the borough.

Issues Raised:

  • Concerns about the plan not adequately addressing pollution from aircraft.
  • Some believe the plan doesn't go far enough or act quickly enough.
  • Suggestions that electric vehicles may not sufficiently reduce pollution due to production emissions and particulate matter from tires.
  • Worries that the plan may negatively impact the disabled and elderly who rely on cars.

2. Aspects to Prioritize

Common Points Raised:

  • Focus on Active Travel: Prioritize developing cycling infrastructure and pedestrianization.
  • Reduce Car Dependency: Emphasize reducing car usage, especially for short journeys and school runs.
  • Enforce Anti-Idling: Target idling vehicles, particularly near schools and congested areas.
  • Tackle Wood Burning: Address pollution from wood burners and multi-fuel stoves.
  • Improve Public Transport: Enhance bus and train services to make them more appealing alternatives to car use.

Good Suggestions:

  • Implement more 20 mph speed limits across the borough.
  • Prioritize areas around schools to protect children from pollution.
  • Increase enforcement against idling engines with real penalties.
  • Develop ambitious targets to reduce overall car usage in Richmond.

Issues Raised:

  • Lack of clarity on why certain areas are prioritized over others.
  • Concerns that current measures like "School Streets" may inadvertently increase pollution elsewhere.
  • Some feel the plan overemphasizes traffic-related pollution and neglects other sources like wood burners and aircraft.

3. Additional Locations Suggested

Common Points Raised:

  • Barnes High Street and White Hart Lane: Noted for congestion and pollution; suggestions to pedestrianize or limit traffic.
  • Mortlake Rail Crossing: Identified as a hotspot for idling engines and pollution.
  • Richmond Bridge and East Twickenham High Street: Need for better cycling infrastructure and pollution mitigation.
  • Hampton Wick and Surrounding Areas: Concerns about increased traffic and idling due to road closures.
  • Areas Near Schools: Emphasis on locations with high child pedestrian traffic for targeted action.

Good Suggestions:

  • Implement traffic reduction measures in identified congested areas.
  • Enhance cycling and pedestrian routes in these locations.
  • Install clearer signage and enforce anti-idling zones at critical points like level crossings.

Issues Raised:

  • Some areas are experiencing increased congestion due to traffic being rerouted from other projects.
  • Lack of infrastructure for safe cycling deters people from choosing active travel options.

4. Further Specific Actions for Target Locations

Common Points Raised:

  • Enforce Anti-Idling: Specifically at Mortlake Rail crossing and near schools.
  • Reevaluate Traffic Schemes: Some existing programs like "School Streets" may be increasing pollution elsewhere.
  • Improve Cycling Infrastructure: Suggestions for cycle lanes over Richmond Bridge and allowing cycling in one-way streets.
  • Reopen Roads: Consider reopening through routes in parks to reduce congestion in populated areas.

Good Suggestions:

  • Introduce cycle ramps on footbridges to enhance accessibility.
  • Adjust traffic light sequencing to improve flow and reduce idling.
  • Plant trees and create green barriers in high pollution areas.

Issues Raised:

  • Some actions may have unintended consequences, such as increased traffic in residential areas.
  • Need for data-driven evaluation of existing programs to assess their actual impact on air quality.

5. Additional Transport Measures Proposed

Common Points Raised:

  • Electrify Public Transport: Calls for more electric buses and trams to reduce emissions.
  • Improve Bus Services: Expand routes, increase frequency, and make public transport more reliable.
  • Introduce 20 mph Speed Limits: To improve safety and reduce emissions from stop-start traffic.
  • Ban or Limit SUVs and Highly Polluting Vehicles: Suggestions to impose levies or restrict their use in the borough.

Good Suggestions:

  • Provide incentives for purchasing e-bikes and using car-sharing schemes.
  • Reinstate bus lanes and remove barriers that slow down public transport.
  • Implement cargo bikes for last-mile deliveries to reduce commercial traffic.

Issues Raised:

  • Concerns that electric vehicles may not fully address pollution due to manufacturing emissions.
  • Some believe that current measures inconvenience motorists without significant benefits.
  • Calls to consider the needs of disabled and elderly residents who rely on cars.

6. Additional Groups to Target

Common Points Raised:

  • Parents on School Runs: Targeting those who drive children to school unnecessarily.
  • Drivers of SUVs and Polluting Vehicles: Focus on those contributing most to emissions.
  • Businesses and Delivery Services: Encourage greener practices and use of cargo bikes.
  • Elderly and Disabled: Ensure policies consider their needs and do not exclude them.

Good Suggestions:

  • Implement educational programs to promote active travel among school children and parents.
  • Provide incentives for businesses to adopt cleaner transport methods.

Issues Raised:

  • Potential social exclusion if policies do not account for the mobility needs of the elderly and disabled.
  • Some respondents feel over-targeted groups may experience undue stress or penalties.

7. Other Areas of Action Suggested

Common Points Raised:

  • Ban Wood Burning Stoves: Due to their significant contribution to particulate pollution.
  • Address Aircraft Pollution: Take action against noise and emissions from air traffic over the borough.
  • Improve Air Quality Monitoring: Increase the number of monitoring stations across the borough.
  • Enhance Green Spaces: Plant more trees and create green barriers to absorb pollutants.

Good Suggestions:

  • Utilize citizen science initiatives to engage the community in air quality monitoring.
  • Work with other councils and the Mayor of London to address larger issues like aircraft pollution.

Issues Raised:

  • Some believe the council is wasting taxpayer money on unnecessary schemes.
  • Concerns that actions in one area may negatively impact another; need for holistic planning.

8. Additional Evidence Requested

Common Points Raised:

  • Health Impact Data: Requests for evidence linking current air quality levels to health outcomes.
  • Air Quality Measurements: Desire for more localized and frequent air quality data.
  • Impact of Aircraft Pollution: Evidence on pollution levels contributed by air traffic.
  • Effectiveness of Current Measures: Data on the actual impact of implemented policies like "School Streets."

Good Suggestions:

  • Collect statistics from GPs and hospitals on respiratory illnesses related to air quality.
  • Provide infographics and dashboards to convey air quality data to the public effectively.

Issues Raised:

  • Some respondents doubt the severity of air quality issues and request concrete evidence.
  • Need for transparent reporting on pollution sources and levels throughout the borough.

9. Further Comments

Common Sentiments Expressed:

  • Support for Ambitious Action: Many appreciate the council's efforts but urge more decisive and faster action.
  • Concerns About Implementation: Skepticism about whether the proposed measures will be effectively implemented and enforced.
  • Desire for Holistic Planning: Calls for considering the interconnectedness of policies and avoiding negative spillover effects.
  • Need for Community Engagement: Suggestions to involve residents more actively in planning and monitoring efforts.

Additional Issues Raised:

  • Impact on Vulnerable Populations: Ensuring that policies do not disadvantage the disabled, elderly, or economically disadvantaged.
  • Criticism of Certain Measures: Some feel that actions like low traffic neighborhoods (LTNs) and 20 mph zones may increase congestion and pollution elsewhere.
  • Calls for Evidence-Based Policies: Emphasis on using data and research to guide decisions and demonstrate effectiveness.

Holistic Summary

The survey responses reflect a community deeply concerned about air quality and supportive of initiatives to improve it. Common themes include a strong desire to reduce car usage, promote active travel through better cycling and walking infrastructure, and enforce measures against idling vehicles. Many respondents advocate for more ambitious and immediate action, emphasizing that current plans may not go far enough to address pressing issues.

There is a significant call to address pollution sources beyond road traffic, notably wood-burning stoves and aircraft emissions. Respondents highlight the need for holistic planning to ensure that actions in one area do not adversely affect another. The importance of considering the needs of vulnerable groups, such as the disabled and elderly, is also emphasized to prevent social exclusion.

Concerns about the effectiveness and enforcement of proposed measures are prevalent, with some skepticism about whether the plans will lead to tangible improvements. Many suggest that policies should be evidence-based, with transparent reporting and community involvement to build trust and ensure accountability.

Overall, the community appears eager to support and participate in efforts to improve air quality but seeks more decisive action, better communication, and consideration of broader impacts to ensure the success of the Richmond Air Quality Action Plan.
