Analysis of Survey Responses

1. Are there any other approaches you would like to suggest?

  • Reduced Car Usage: Many favour stricter measures to discourage driving, such as restricting or banning SUVs, creating car-free streets, and increasing parking fees.
  • Wood-Burning Controls: Multiple calls to ban or heavily regulate domestic wood-burners, citing severe particulate pollution and health risks.
  • Strong Active Travel Proposals: Suggestions for extensive segregated cycle lanes, more pedestrian areas, and safer pavements to cut reliance on cars.
  • Enforcement & Education: Respondents propose higher fines for idling and speeding, plus awareness campaigns to shift public behaviour.
  • Heathrow & Aircraft Emissions: Some stress the need for concerted lobbying to tackle noise and air pollution from flights overhead.
  • 20mph vs. 30mph Debate: Mixed views; some want universal 20mph for safety and cleaner air, others believe 20mph can increase congestion and pollution.
  • Park Re-openings & Traffic Rerouting: Divided opinions on Bushy Park and other closures, with some asserting displacement of traffic to residential roads.

2. Are there any aspects of the plan you think should be prioritised?

  • School Streets & Idling: Many insist reducing car presence around schools should be paramount, citing children’s heightened vulnerability.
  • Active Travel & Safe Cycling: Strong appetite for prioritising fully protected bike lanes, pedestrian routes, and reduced car space in town centres.
  • Strict Enforcement: Respondents urge no-idling penalties and consistent crackdowns on speeding, engine revving, and illegal parking.
  • Wood-Burning Enforcement: Repeated pressure to target wood burners, with some wanting immediate bans or rigorous oversight.
  • Bus Electrification: Calls to accelerate conversion to electric or zero-emission buses, particularly on busy or congested routes.
  • Major Junctions & Hotspots: Many mention Mortlake rail crossing, Hampton Wick High Street, Chalkers Corner, and Barnes High Street as areas needing urgent action.

3. If there are any additional locations you would suggest, please tell us here.

  • Mortlake, White Hart Lane, & Barnes: High congestion and idling near level crossings. Fears of rising pollution as local developments progress.
  • Hampton & Hampton Hill: Gridlock around the High Street and pinch points leading to worsening air quality. Requests for expanded bus routes.
  • Teddington & Twickenham Corridors: Busy roads (A316, London Road) cause concern, particularly at peak times. Some want 20mph or improved cycle paths.
  • Richmond Town Centre & Bridge Approaches: Frequent suggestions to pedestrianise or restrict through-traffic to cut queues and idle engines.
  • Areas Under Flight Paths: Recurring mention of aircraft noise and emissions over large swathes of the borough.

4. If there are any other actions you suggest specific to a target location, please tell us below.

  • Pinch-Point Remodelling: Redesign busy junctions (e.g., Hampton, Mortlake crossing) to reduce queues and encourage smoother traffic flow.
  • Pedestrianising Streets: Multiple calls to close sections of Barnes, Richmond, or Twickenham town centres to private cars, especially on weekends or permanently.
  • Better Signage & Idling Fines: Targeting schools, stations, and level crossings with visible anti-idling notices and robust enforcement measures.
  • “Green Walls” & Urban Planting: Proposals to plant more hedges, trees, and living walls near traffic hotspots to help filter pollutants.
  • Mixed Views on Park Access: Some want roads in Bushy Park reopened on weekdays to spread traffic, others wish to keep them closed to preserve air quality there.

5. If there are any additional transport measures you wish to propose, please tell us here.

  • Better Cycling Infrastructure: Numerous demands for continuous, protected cycle lanes, safe parking (covered and free), and e-bike charging points.
  • Radical Car-Free Initiatives: Ideas such as car-free days, bans on non-resident traffic, and last-mile walking or cycling for school runs.
  • Electrifying Public Transport: Some advocate trams or new rail routes; many want quicker bus electrification plus more frequent, reliable bus services.
  • Disincentivising Car Ownership: Proposals to raise parking permit costs (especially for second cars, large SUVs), reduce on-street parking space, and shift road capacity to cyclists or pedestrians.
  • Scepticism about EVs: While many want expanded EV charging, others note heavier EVs generate more PM from tyres and brakes.

6. If there are any additional groups you think should be targeted, please tell us here.

  • School-Run Parents: Regarded as key contributors to congestion and idling, especially during drop-off and pick-up times.
  • Older & Disabled Residents: Concerns that heavy restrictions may isolate those reliant on cars. Some call for better inclusive transport solutions.
  • Delivery Drivers & Contractors: Vans idling on kerbs, online deliveries causing repeated short trips. Calls for more enforcement or cargo-bike incentives.
  • Wood-Burner Users: Seen as a group needing particular outreach and/or sanctions to reduce particulate pollution.
  • Large Vehicle Owners: SUV drivers singled out for both emissions and occupying excessive road/pavement space.

7. If you have any other suggestions for areas of action, please tell us here.

  • Heathrow & Aircraft Noise: Some strongly emphasise the need to address or lobby against existing and expanded flights, referencing both noise and NOx.
  • Wood-Burning & Solid Fuel Use: Repeated calls for swift bans, especially in built-up zones, not just “education” about pollution dangers.
  • Large Scale Projects: Worries that construction works (e.g., Thames Water, big redevelopments) could hamper local air quality; urge thorough oversight and planning.
  • Noise Pollution: References to e-bikes, scooters, leaf blowers, and also large outdoor events. Some want stronger by-laws and enforcement.
  • Green Spaces & Urban Trees: Calls for more greenery in public areas, alongside demands that existing trees be properly maintained.

8. If there is any additional evidence you would like to see, please tell us here.

  • More Local Monitoring: Many request additional air-quality monitors, especially in residential areas near main roads and level crossings.
  • Health Impact Data: Calls for closer links to GP and hospital admissions data to illustrate local pollution’s real effects, e.g., on asthma or cardiovascular issues.
  • Breakdown of Pollution Sources: Especially curious about the relative contributions from aviation, cars, wood stoves, and heavier vehicles.
  • Comparative Studies & Best Practice: Some want examples from European cities to show successful interventions (e.g., Utrecht, Paris).
  • Evidence of Enforcement: Several suggest publishing penalty data for idling or speeding to show follow-through and deter offenders.

9. Please use this space for any further comments about the Richmond Air Quality Action Plan.

  • “Not Radical Enough” vs. “Anti-Car”: A notable split between those wanting swifter, firmer action and those who see the plan as unfairly penalising drivers.
  • Lack of Specific Targets: Respondents often criticise the plan for being high-level but missing measurable goals (e.g., “x km of cycle lanes” by a certain date).
  • Implementation Doubts: Many suspect enforcement will remain weak, and mention frustration with previously unheeded complaints about idling or speeding.
  • Equity Concerns: Anxiety that older or disabled residents are left behind if car restrictions multiply. Some emphasise cost barriers to EV adoption.
  • Positive Mentions & Willingness to Engage: Several praise the Council for consulting on this topic, want to volunteer time or ideas, but stress the need for “actual changes” on the ground.

Holistic Summary

Overall, there is strong consensus that air quality is a high-priority issue which demands meaningful actions and enforcement. Yet respondents diverge on how aggressive the measures should be and how to balance differing needs. Many advocate radical traffic reduction, mass pedestrianisation, and outright bans on wood-burners, while others caution that “anti-car” policies can penalise older or disabled people and shift pollution onto surrounding roads.

Common proposals include robust expansions of cycling infrastructure, more stringent controls on idling vehicles (especially near schools and level crossings), and direct lobbying to curb aircraft emissions from Heathrow. Wood-burning stoves repeatedly come under fire as a major local pollutant needing firm regulation. At the same time, there are persistent calls for better data, clearer targets, and credible enforcement — particularly for no-idling rules, speeding, and parking infractions.

In summary, the community appears supportive of reducing pollution in principle, but has strong opinions on the best ways forward. Recurrent themes include a need for more detailed planning, full transparency over data, faster bus electrification, and real investment in alternatives (such as e-bike infrastructure) to ensure driving remains an option of last resort rather than the default.
